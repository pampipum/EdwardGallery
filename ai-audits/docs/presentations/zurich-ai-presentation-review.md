# Zurich Presentation Review

Source reviewed:

- https://gist.github.com/pampipum/7b875317326fe4405c7068b6630edaf5

## Overall Read

The presentation has strong energy and a clear thesis:

- AI is shifting from chat to execution
- Microsoft covers the office boundary
- custom skills and internal bridges create the real moat

That core logic is strong for a Zurich business audience.

## What Works Well

- clear ladder from productivity to agentic execution
- strong distinction between RAG and agents
- concrete company-skill examples
- governance warning near the end
- 90-day call to action is practical

## What Needs Tightening

Some claims are too aggressive or too absolute for a Swiss business audience and should be softened or sourced.

### 1. ARC-AGI-2 slide

Risk:

- the exact leaderboard claims in the notes mention models and scores that should be verified live before presenting

Safer framing:

- frontier models are improving rapidly on reasoning-oriented benchmarks such as ARC-AGI-2
- the important point is not one exact score but that reasoning quality is improving fast enough to unlock more structured workflow use

### 2. OpenClaw slide

Risk:

- phrases like "most-starred project in GitHub history", "surpassed Linux", and the China installation story are high-risk claims unless you can show a source on-screen

Recommendation:

- either source every number precisely or reduce the slide to a broader market-signal point:
  - agentic AI tooling is seeing unusually fast open-source adoption
  - the market is validating action-taking AI, not just chat

### 3. Microsoft data boundary slide

Risk:

- "your data does not leave your company" is too absolute for a cloud product

Safer framing:

- Microsoft positions Copilot Cowork as operating within the Microsoft 365 security and governance boundary
- prompts, permissions, and tenant controls remain governed inside the organization’s Microsoft environment
- the exact data-handling model should be validated against Microsoft documentation and tenant configuration

### 4. Open models / on-prem slide

Risk:

- "the answer can finally be yes" is rhetorically good but can sound too casual for regulated Swiss buyers

Safer framing:

- open-weight and cost-efficient models make on-premise or tightly controlled deployment more realistic for selected workloads
- whether that is appropriate depends on data sensitivity, infrastructure, and governance requirements

## Swiss / Zurich Tone Adjustments

For Zurich, especially with technical or executive audiences, cut some of the dramatic language:

- replace "undeniable market signal" with "clear market signal"
- replace "tireless architectural peers" with "high-leverage engineering systems"
- replace "the world is adopting at a pace software has never seen" with "adoption is accelerating unusually quickly"

## Best Closing Angle

Keep the ending grounded in business reality:

- where is the workflow friction
- where is the data already structured
- where is human review acceptable
- where can one pilot produce visible value in 90 days

That is the strongest Swiss framing.

## Suggested Core Message

Do not sell AI as magic.

Sell it as:

- a new execution layer
- bounded by governance
- strongest when connected to your proprietary workflows
- most valuable when it removes repeatable friction without removing accountability
